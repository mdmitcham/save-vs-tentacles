There is more to Homeric epic than the shedding of blood.

## Sorcery. Prophecy. Leadership.

You don't want to play a sorceress who can polymorph enemeis into resentful yet  compliant pigs? You don't want to shriek prophecy at the disbelieving masses and be run out of town? Or lead a nearly incontrollable warband which is at turns murderous and stupid? Fuck you.

Plus I wanted to experiment.

I didn't want to hand every character the knight's feat system and then pile a subsystem on top of it. First, it would make characters too "powerful" or "unbalanced." And by that I mean that they would outshine other characters in every domain. Balance is not really a worry except for when it negates the contributions of other players.

And what I landed on is that if I wanted the Sorceress and  the Prophet to have their own flavor and be somewhat balanced then I would have to rip out their feats and replace them.

So they don't get Smite, Deny, or Focus. They get their own "things" instead.

## Wondrous and Horrible Spells of the Path of Mageia

I ripped the list from various Glog and Knave sources. Then I just bent it to fit the setting. In the OSR magic is a problem solving / utility tool. So we've got charms. We've got invisibility. We've got polymorph. We've got self to snakes.

Spells — Roll 1d20 three times, reroll duplicates:

1. **Anthropomorphize** — Grant an animal human intelligence or form for a day.
2. **Charm** — The creature you cast this on treats you as a friend, and you may roll again to affect another creature (up to 3).
3. **Whisper of Hypnos** — A creature falls into a light sleep, and you may roll again to affect another creature (up to 3).
4. **Dagger of Hypnos** — A creature answers a single yes or no question.
5. **Phobia** — A creature becomes terrified of an object or person of your choice and cannot move nearer to them, and you can roll again to affect additional creatures (up to three)
6. **Apprehend** — You can hear the surface thoughts of nearby creatures.
7. **Skullshade** — You can spend a watch weaving this spell on the skull of a recently dead human, after which it gains semi-permanent undeath and can deliver one sentence a day.
8. **Raise the Slain** — A recently slain corpse rises to serve you, with Vig 10, Cla 3, Spi 12, Gd 3.
9. **Vision** — Completely control what a creature sees.
10. **Muse Display** — An impressive illusory display up to palace-sized appears and seems real until physical investigation.
11. **Command the Ligneous Masses** — Nearby plants obey you and can move.
12. **Polymorph** — Turn a human into an animal with a VIG save to resist. They revert back if they are harmed.
13. **Self to Snake** — You can become a snake (Gd 1, Vig 10, Cla 10, Spi 5) and revert back when struck or at will.
14. **Illuminate** — A floating light moves at your command.
15. **Shroud of Kthonia** — A creature becomes invisible until they move.
16. **Smoke Form** — Your body becomes living smoke.
17. **Swarm** — Become a swarm of birds, spiders, or fish. You can barely think in swarm form and have headaches afterward.
18. **Weave of Silk** — Thick, near-impassable webbing appears and covers an area encompassing no more than 1d6 creatures.
19. **Spirit Riding** — Have a spirit inhabit your body in exchange for some favor. You completely lose control until dawn.
20. **Dream** — While sleeping, enter the dreams of a human you have interacted with, and you can deliver a message or terrifying nightmare.

### Roll to Retain

Spells work like feats. You roll to retain them.

Cast the spell --> Clarity roll --> spell goes off --> on a failure it is lost.

Lost spells are gone until Clarity returns.

(I changed monuments so they restore Clarity, not spirit).

(And because you can restore clarity with Stimulant RAW, it incentivizes the sorceress to run around with a various esoteric Stimulants and weird substances).

### In which the table learns the subtle and powerful intricacies of  Charm

Anserotaurs are violent and grotesque beasts straight from the  fires of Tartarous. Part horse, part enraged goose, they have an elongated neck made for bellowing, beady evil eyes for detecting innocent prey, two rows of serrated teeth, and protruding from their chest, between 2-12 little grippers that look like baby hands. Some also breathe fire.

The party murdered a whole family of them and chased down and stole their eggs when the eggs sprouded feet and ran off. Then they charmed the last anserotaur and used it as a war-mount.

Awesome.

But obviously I couldn't have them doing that with various high powered enemies because MB is kind of an OP curbstomp ANYWAY, and I wanted them to still have to engage with the game instead of having the sorceress just throw minion after minion at the problem.

So now we have some RuLinGs nOt RULES about how mind control works.

Charm spells can't be cast in combat. Named characters, though not namess dregs, get a save against it.

AND if you ever *lose* Charm by failing the Clarity roll after casting it every creature currently under it breaks free at the same moment.

And they know.

They know exactly what you did to them.

So sure, you CAN build a ~~slave empire~~ network of charmed allies and you CAN charm the king...

But tick tock motherfucker.

With these new rules the sorceress was attempting to charm her way past an encamped warband and the player said his hands were getting sweaty because a single failed roll could mean the entire warband turned on them.

# The Path of Prophecy

Prophecy isn't magic. Prophets have prophetic Visions instead. They can use one per day. There's no roll.

## Mythic Vision

The first time you encounter any myth, roll 1d6. On a 1–3, you get the name of the myth. On a 4–5, you also get the rules of the next omen. On a 6, you understand whether failing to resolve the myth spells weal, woe, or uncertain. Oracles mistrust you.

## Vision of War

When entering battle, roll CLA on 2d20. If both rolls succeed, proclaim one being who will die and one who will live — the fates will intervene to make it so, though the outcome is still not certain. If one succeeds and one fails, proclaim one who will live OR one who will die — the fates will intervene. If both fail, lose 1d6 CLA.

## Vision of the Future

Cast bones or sticks and beseech the fates to reveal your near future. You gain a vision of the outcome of your current plan.

## Behold the lunacy of our prophet

Our prophet became a madman.

It was actually 🧑‍🍳 chefs kiss 🧑‍🍳.

Because they had beheld mythic visions (of the Sun myth specifically) and got the rules of the next omen, they were looking for a pair of ragamuffin thieves. And when that omen occured, they fell upon that couple of petty vegetable poachers and threatened them at knifepoint, raving about moles, Apollyon, and the setting sun.

It was exactly what a crazy person would do. But then again, he was acting on information only he could see.

They eventually bound one of those poor people to a shipmast and sacrificed them to Poseidus, and they took another to the temple of Apollyon and gutted and dismembered them in a most upsetting manner, with much deranged chanting about the Sun.

I never once felt the urge to give prophets a feat. Their power is information, not violence. I think this is a fit for most ItO games and their approach to information design.

# The Path of Command

This was the one I was most concerned about. The verdict is still out. Here's their main "core" ability:

You have a Warband. When they enter a peaceful situation, roll 1d6 for their reaction.

Their reaction:

1–3 — Someone in your Warband has their own goals here, and simply MUST pick a fight, help someone, fall in love, steal something, call in a real or imagined debt, or fulfill some other impulse.
4–5 — As 1–3, but your Warband looks to you first to see if it's okay.
6 — All is well.

Basically, they are a military leader who is constantly chafing under the weight of rule and their dumbfuck soldiers. They DO have access to feats, which with the warband makes them quite a terror in combat. But I'm not worried about that. Particularly vicious can always be tuned to fight on par with a warband.

I'm designing the balance to come from unpredictability. You can't always rely on your warband. They will probably get you into trouble. They might even work against you.

"Select three individuals — your most naive, your most temperamental, and your most ambitious — and detail with names."

Come on. A naive follower, a temperamental follower, and an ambitious follower will are absolutely going to be more trouble than they're worth.

That's the goal anyway.

If this bears any similarities to the Harrier playbook in Apocalypse World, which I definitely did not run a 6-session campaign for, and it was awesome, and the Harrier kept using their warband to blow holes in shit, I can assure you this is completely coincidental.
